What Does Time, Times, And Half A Time Mean?

Daniel and Revelation repeat the same prophetic period as time, times, and half a time, 42 months, and 1260 days, pointing to a long era of persecution.

Daniel 7:25Daniel 12:7Revelation 11:2-3Revelation 12:6Revelation 12:14Revelation 13:5

The phrase is repeated

Time, times, and half a time appears in Daniel 7 and Daniel 12. Revelation repeats the same period in parallel forms: 1260 days and 42 months. The repetition shows that this is not a minor detail.

The period belongs to symbolic apocalyptic settings with beasts, horns, persecution, wilderness, and final conflict. That context matters for interpretation.

The year-day pattern

Numbers 14:34 and Ezekiel 4:6 show that prophetic symbolism can use a day for a year. This does not mean every biblical day should be read as a year. It means the year-day pattern is appropriate when the context is symbolic and prophetic.

In Daniel and Revelation, the 1260-day period appears in precisely that kind of context. It describes the long career of a persecuting religious-political power, not a brief literal three-and-a-half-year episode only.

The historical period

The 1260 years are commonly connected with the period of papal supremacy from A.D. 538 to 1798. This period fits Daniel 7's little horn, which arises from the divided Roman world, differs from ordinary kingdoms, speaks against God, persecutes the saints, and thinks to change times and law.

The endpoint around 1798 also fits Revelation's wound language and Daniel 11's time-of-the-end movement.

The period is about more than dates

The time period is not given merely to satisfy chronological curiosity. It assures God's people that persecution is limited. The horn may wear out the saints, but heaven has measured the period.

Daniel 7 moves from the horn's time to the heavenly court. Revelation moves from persecution to final witness and judgment-hour proclamation. The date serves the larger message.

Why this matters

Time, times, and half a time teaches that God knows the length of oppression and sets its limit. The persecuting power does not rule forever.

The period also helps connect Daniel and Revelation as one prophetic story, moving from Rome's divided world to final judgment and deliverance.
