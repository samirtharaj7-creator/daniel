Introduction

Introduction to the Book of Daniel

Daniel is not mainly a puzzle book. It is a witness to the God who rules history, reveals truth, judges evil, preserves His people, and brings the kingdoms of this world to the kingdom of Christ.

Name

Daniel means "God is my judge."

Author

The book presents Daniel as an exile, court servant, and prophet.

Setting

Judah in exile under Babylon and early Medo-Persia.

Structure

Stories of faithfulness, then visions of history and the end.

Message

God rules history, reveals truth, judges evil, and delivers His people.

01

The purpose of Daniel

The book is named after Daniel, the Hebrew exile who becomes a faithful court servant, interpreter of dreams, prophet, and public witness before pagan kings. His name means “God is my judge”, and that meaning becomes one of the theological keys to the whole book.

Daniel is about beasts, horns, kings, and timelines, but it is never only about those things. It is about the living God who judges, rules, reveals, delivers, humbles empires, and forms a people who can stand when loyalty is costly.

The narratives teach the practical life of the judgment hour: temperance, prayer, worship, courage, humility, and public faithfulness. The visions explain why that life matters in the final conflict.
02

Authorship, date, and setting

Daniel begins in the Babylonian crisis. Jerusalem is under pressure, temple vessels are carried to Babylon, and selected young Hebrews are trained for imperial service. The book presents Daniel as one of those exiles, a man who lives through the Babylonian period and continues serving into the early Medo-Persian period.

The opening date places Daniel in the first wave of Babylonian pressure on Judah, commonly placed in 605 B.C. From there the book moves through Nebuchadnezzar, Belshazzar, Darius the Mede, and Cyrus. That historical span matters because Daniel is not presented as a distant observer looking backward; he is presented as a witness inside the courts, crises, and visions he records.

That setting matters. Daniel is not written as detached philosophy; it is rooted in exile, court pressure, threatened worship, foreign education, empire, prayer, visions, and hope. Its historical movement runs from Babylon to Medo-Persia, then its prophecies look beyond Daniel's own lifetime toward later kingdoms, judgment, and final deliverance.

605 B.C.

Jerusalem is subdued; Daniel and other young Hebrews are taken to Babylon.

539 B.C.

Babylon falls and Medo-Persia becomes the ruling world power.

536/535 B.C.

Daniel receives the final vision in the third year of Cyrus.

2nd-1st century B.C.

Copies of Daniel among the Dead Sea Scrolls show early circulation and reception.

1st century A.D.

Jesus refers to Daniel as a prophet when speaking about the abomination of desolation.

03

Daniel and early authorship

The book itself presents Daniel as the authorial and prophetic figure behind the visions. Daniel 1 introduces him as an exile taken to Babylon. Daniel 7:1 says Daniel had a dream and wrote the sum of the matters. Daniel 8:1, Daniel 9:2, Daniel 10:2, and Daniel 12:5 continue to place the visions in Daniel's own experience.

The biblical witness also treats Daniel as more than a literary character. Ezekiel names Daniel as a known righteous and wise figure, and Jesus refers to Daniel as a prophet when speaking about the abomination of desolation. Those witnesses do not answer every modern question by themselves, but they are important evidence for how Scripture identifies Daniel.

The internal details also fit the world the book describes. Daniel 5 knows that Belshazzar could offer only the third place in the kingdom, a detail that makes sense when Nabonidus stands above Belshazzar in Babylon's final administration. The book's mixture of Hebrew and Aramaic also follows its movement: covenant identity, world-empire testimony, then renewed focus on God's people, sanctuary, Messiah, and final deliverance.

The book's own presentation

Daniel is introduced as a young exile from Judah, serves in Babylon, continues into the Persian period, and receives first-person visions in Daniel 7-12.

Jesus' witness

Jesus speaks of Daniel as a prophet when referring to the abomination of desolation, treating Daniel as a real prophetic voice.

Ezekiel's witness

Ezekiel names Daniel alongside Noah and Job as a known righteous figure, and also associates Daniel with exceptional wisdom.

Belshazzar detail

Daniel 5 knows Belshazzar's Babylonian role and explains why Daniel could be made only the third ruler in the kingdom.

Language and setting

The Hebrew-Aramaic structure fits a book moving from covenant identity to world-empire concerns and back to Israel, sanctuary, Messiah, and the end; the court setting also fits Babylonian and Persian realities.

Early manuscript evidence

Daniel was copied and valued early enough to appear among the Dead Sea Scrolls, showing that the book had already gained wide religious authority before the time of Christ.

Some place Daniel in the second century B.C. during the Antiochus crisis. The evidence above points in the other direction: Daniel is presented as a sixth-century exile, is treated as a prophet by Jesus, contains court details that fit Babylonian-Persian realities, and was already circulating early enough to be copied among the Dead Sea Scrolls.
04

The shape of the book

Daniel combines court narrative and apocalyptic prophecy. Daniel 1-6 tells stories of faithful witness in exile. Daniel 7-12 gives symbolic visions and angelic explanations involving beasts, horns, heavenly beings, time periods, sanctuary, judgment, the Messiah, Michael, resurrection, and final deliverance.

Daniel 1

The exile begins with identity

The book opens with defeat, but not with divine absence. Daniel 1 introduces Jerusalem, Babylon, conscience, appetite, education, and the quiet resolve of young believers who refuse to let empire rename their loyalties.

Daniel 2-6

The court stories train faithfulness

Dreams, fire, pride, judgment, prayer, and lions show how God's people live under pressure. The stories are not moral ornaments; they prepare the reader for the prophetic crises that follow.

Daniel 7-12

The visions reveal the conflict behind history

Beasts, horns, books, sanctuary language, the Messiah, Michael, resurrection, and final deliverance disclose a larger great-controversy struggle moving toward God's everlasting kingdom.

Daniel is written in Hebrew and Aramaic. The language shift matters because the book moves from covenant identity, to the broad world-empires, and back to God's people, sanctuary, Messiah, and the end.

Daniel 1:1-2:4a

Hebrew - Judah, Jerusalem, exile, and covenant identity.

Daniel 2:4b-7:28

Aramaic - A broad world-empire focus for the nations.

Daniel 8:1-12:13

Hebrew - God's people, sanctuary, Messiah, and the end.

05

Major themes

The stories and visions interpret one another. The stories show how God's people live under empire; the visions show why they must endure. Daniel 1 teaches faithfulness in appetite and identity. Daniel 3 teaches faithfulness in worship. Daniel 6 teaches faithfulness in prayer. The later visions reveal the larger conflict behind these tests.

06

Daniel and Revelation belong together

Daniel and Revelation are companion books. Both contain prophetic lines extending from the prophet's day toward the last days, and Revelation repeatedly opens, extends, and applies Daniel's symbols.

Daniel's beasts reappear in Revelation's sea beast. Daniel's judgment books connect with Revelation's heavenly court and book of life. Michael, resurrection language, sealing, opened revelation, sanctuary themes, and final deliverance all draw the two books together.

Daniel 7 → Revelation 13

Beasts, horns, blasphemy, and persecuting power.

Daniel 8-9 → Revelation 11; 14

Sanctuary, judgment, worship, and final warning.

Daniel 12 → Revelation 10; 22

Sealed and opened revelation, waiting, and final hope.

07

Sanctuary and judgment

Daniel's sanctuary and judgment themes become central in Daniel 7-9. Daniel 7 shows a heavenly court scene where books are opened. Daniel 8 introduces the 2300 evenings-mornings and the cleansing or restoration of the sanctuary. Daniel 9 connects the seventy weeks to the Messiah and to the larger Daniel 8 framework.

Daniel's judgment language is not abstract. Books are opened, the sanctuary is restored, Messiah is central, and evil does not keep the final word. The visions press toward God setting things right through judgment and giving the kingdom to the Son of Man and His people.
08

How the prophetic lines fit together

Daniel's visions do not stand as isolated puzzles. They repeat and expand the same basic movement: present empire, succeeding kingdoms, opposition to God's people, heavenly judgment, and the kingdom God establishes. Daniel 2 gives the broad outline; Daniel 7 repeats it with beasts and judgment; Daniel 8 narrows the focus to sanctuary conflict; Daniel 9 supplies the Messiah-centered time key; Daniel 10-12 carries the conflict to final deliverance.

Daniel 2

Image of metals - Babylon to divided Rome, then God's kingdom.

Daniel 7

Four beasts and judgment - Little horn, heavenly court, Son of Man, and saints.

Daniel 8

Ram, goat, little horn - Sanctuary conflict and the 2300 evenings-mornings.

Daniel 9

Seventy weeks - Messiah, covenant, and the larger Daniel 8 framework.

Daniel 10-12

Final vision - North/south conflict, Michael, trouble, resurrection, and hope.

The book should be read as a whole. The court stories teach faithfulness under pressure, and the visions explain the historical and heavenly conflict behind that pressure. Daniel's prophecy gives evidence that God knows the future, rules over kingdoms, and will vindicate truth.

Some details in Daniel 11 require careful study, but the larger line is clear: earthly powers rise and fall, worship becomes the central issue, Michael stands for His people, and God's final deliverance closes the book.
09

Begin studying Daniel

The best way to read Daniel is to move from the setting into the text itself. Begin with Daniel 1 and watch the book build its themes: faithfulness, conscience, prayer, worship, God's sovereignty, prophetic symbols, historical fulfillment, sanctuary, judgment, Messiah, and final deliverance.
